Common Attacks Explained

What Are Cyber Attacks?

Cyber attacks are when criminals try to steal your business data, lock your files, or trick you into giving them money. They target small businesses because you often have valuable information but fewer security protections than big companies.

Most Common Attacks (And How to Stop Them)

1. Phishing (Fake Emails & Messages)
What it is:

Criminals send fake emails, texts, or calls pretending to be your bank, supplier, or even your boss to steal passwords or money.

How to spot it:
  • Urgent language like "Act now or your account will be closed"
  • Asking for passwords, bank details, or immediate payments
  • Links that don't match the real website (hover over them to check)
  • Generic greetings like "Dear Customer" instead of your name
What to do:
  1. Stop and verify: Don't click anything. Call the company directly using a phone number you already have saved
  2. Check the sender: Click on the sender's name to see the full email address, scammers often use fake domains
  3. Report and delete: Forward suspicious emails to your IT support and delete them
  4. Use verification protocols: We'll cover this in detail later, but for now just remember to verify unusual requests through a different method

2. Ransomware (Locked Files)
What it is:

Malicious software that locks all your files and demands payment to unlock them. It spreads through email attachments, unsafe websites, or infected USB drives.

What to do:
  1. Back up everything: We'll show you exactly how to do this safely - see our backup guide
  2. Don't open suspicious attachments: If you don't recognise the sender or weren't expecting the file, don't open it
  3. Keep software updated: We'll cover automated updates later, read more here
  4. If infected, don't pay: Disconnect from the internet immediately and call a cybersecurity expert

3. Business Email Compromise
What it is:

Criminals impersonate your boss or supplier to trick you into transferring money or sharing sensitive information.

What to do:
  1. Verify all payment requests: Always call the person directly before sending money, even if the email looks legitimate
  2. Set up verification codes: Agree on secret phrases with your bank and key suppliers for phone confirmations
  3. Question unusual requests: If your boss suddenly asks for gift cards or urgent wire transfers, verify through a different method
  4. Use approval processes: We'll cover access management later - read more here
4. AI-Enhanced Attacks
What it is:

Criminals use artificial intelligence to create fake voices, write perfect emails, and make highly personalised attacks that are harder to detect.

What to do:
  1. Never trust voice alone: If someone calls asking for urgent payments, hang up and call them back on a number you already have
  2. Perfect grammar doesn't mean legitimate: AI can now write flawless emails, so don't trust emails just because they're well-written
  3. Limit public information: Review your social media and company website - criminals use this information to personalise attacks
  4. Use verification protocols: We'll cover this in our employee training section - read more here
5. Data Breaches
What it is:

Criminals steal customer information, payment details, or business data by breaking into your systems.

What to do:
  1. Use strong, unique passwords: We'll show you exactly how to do this: read more here
  2. Enable two-factor authentication: This adds an extra security step: see our MFA guide
  3. Limit access: Only give staff access to data they need for their job
  4. Monitor for breaches: Sign up for breach notifications and change passwords immediately if your data is compromised
6. Insider Threats
What it is:

Current or former employees who intentionally or accidentally cause security problems.

What to do:
  1. Remove access immediately: When someone leaves, change passwords and remove their system access the same day
  2. Monitor activity: Keep logs of who accesses what data and when
  3. Train your team: We'll cover security training in detail: read more here
  4. Use the principle of least privilege: Give people only the minimum access they need to do their job
