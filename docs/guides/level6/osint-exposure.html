OSINT Exposure

What is OSINT Exposure?

Open Source Intelligence (OSINT) Exposure refers to how much information about your business, employees, and systems is publicly available online. Cyber criminals use this freely available information to plan targeted attacks against your business.

The more attackers know about your business, the more convincing and effective their attacks become. Reducing your OSINT footprint makes it harder for criminals to craft personalised scams and find vulnerabilities.

Common Sources of Business OSINT

  • Company Website: Staff names, photos, contact details, technology mentions, and organisational structure information.
  • Social Media: Employee personal accounts, company pages, photos of offices, equipment, and business relationships.
  • Professional Networks: LinkedIn profiles revealing job roles, technologies used, and company connections.
  • Job Postings: Technical requirements that reveal what systems and software your business uses.
  • Public Records: Business registrations, property records, and regulatory filings that show business structure and finances.
  • Technical Footprint: Public IP addresses, domain registration details, and exposed services visible through internet scanning.

How Attackers Use OSINT Against Small Business

  • Spear Phishing: Use employee names and roles to create convincing fake emails that appear to come from colleagues or suppliers.
  • Social Engineering: Reference specific business details, relationships, and current projects to build trust during phone calls.
  • Credential Attacks: Target specific systems and applications they know you use based on job postings and technology mentions.
  • Physical Security: Use office photos and location information to plan physical break-ins or tailgating attacks.
  • Supply Chain Attacks: Identify your suppliers and partners to compromise your business through third-party relationships.
  • Executive Targeting: Focus attacks on senior staff using personal information gathered from social media and public records.

Conducting Your Own OSINT Assessment

  • Google Your Business: Search for your company name, domain, and key employees. Look at images, news, and cached pages to see what's publicly available.
  • Check Social Media: Review your company's Facebook, LinkedIn, Instagram, and Twitter accounts. Look for information that could help attackers.
  • Examine Employee Profiles: Check staff LinkedIn profiles for technology mentions, organisational details, and personal information that could be used in attacks.
  • Review Job Postings: Look at your current and historical job advertisements to see what technical details you've revealed about your systems.
  • Scan Your Website: Use tools like Wayback Machine to see historical versions of your website and what information might have been accidentally published.
  • Check Domain Information: Use WHOIS lookup tools to see what registration details are publicly visible for your domain names.

Reducing Your OSINT Footprint

  • Limit Staff Directory Information: Remove or minimise employee contact details on your website. Use general contact forms instead of direct email addresses.
  • Review Social Media Privacy: Set business social media accounts to private where possible and train staff on personal account privacy settings.
  • Sanitise Job Postings: Remove specific technology names and system details from job advertisements. Use general terms instead.
  • Control Photo Sharing: Avoid posting photos that show computer screens, network equipment, or office layouts that could reveal security information.
  • Use Domain Privacy: Enable WHOIS privacy protection for your business domain names to hide registration details.
  • Monitor Your Mentions: Set up Google Alerts for your business name and key employees to know when new information is published online.

Employee OSINT Security Training

  • Personal Social Media: Train staff to review their privacy settings and avoid posting work-related information that could help attackers.
  • Professional Profiles: Guide employees on what information is safe to include in LinkedIn profiles and what should be kept private.
  • Conference and Events: Advise staff to be cautious about sharing business information when speaking at conferences or industry events.
  • Phishing Awareness: Help employees understand how attackers use publicly available information to make phishing emails more convincing.
  • Information Classification: Teach staff to classify information as public, internal, or confidential before sharing it anywhere online.
  • Regular Reviews: Encourage employees to periodically review their online presence and remove or update outdated information.

Simple Steps to Start Today

  • ☐ Google search your business name, domain, and key employees to see what information is publicly available
  • ☐ Review your company website and remove unnecessary employee contact details and technology specifics
  • ☐ Check your business social media accounts and adjust privacy settings to limit public information
  • ☐ Use WHOIS lookup tools to check your domain registration details and enable privacy protection if needed
  • ☐ Set up Google Alerts for your business name and key personnel to monitor new online mentions
  • ☐ Review current job postings and remove specific technology and system details
  • ☐ Train employees on social media privacy settings and professional information sharing best practices
