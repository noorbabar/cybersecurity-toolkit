Complete Phishing Protection Guide

What is Phishing?

Phishing is when cyber criminals pretend to be someone you trust to trick you into handing over sensitive information, money, or access to your systems. It's not just emails anymore - attackers use phone calls, text messages, social media, and even in-person tactics.

Reality: With AI, criminals can now perfectly mimic voices, write flawless emails, and create convincing fake websites in minutes. The old "look for typos" advice no longer works.

95% of successful cyber attacks start with some form of phishing. Small businesses are prime targets because they often lack the sophisticated defenses of large corporations.

Types of Phishing Attacks

Modern phishing comes in many forms. Here are the most common types targeting small businesses:

Email Phishing (Classic)

What it is: Fake emails pretending to be from banks, suppliers, or services you use.

Example: "Your PayPal account is suspended. Click here to reactivate."

Red flags: Urgent language, suspicious sender domains, unexpected requests for information.

Vishing (Voice Phishing)

What it is: Phone calls from scammers pretending to be IT support, banks, or government agencies.

Example: "This is Microsoft support. Your computer is infected. Give us remote access to fix it."

AI Danger: Criminals can now clone voices perfectly using just a few seconds of audio from social media.

Defense: Never give passwords or remote access over the phone. Hang up and call back on official numbers.

Smishing (SMS Phishing)

What it is: Text messages with malicious links or requests for information.

Example: "Your package delivery failed. Click here to reschedule: bit.ly/fake-link"

Business target: Fake messages about invoices, deliveries, or urgent business matters.

Defense: Don't click links in unexpected texts. Verify through official channels.

Spear Phishing

What it is: Highly targeted attacks using personal information about you or your business.

Example: Email to your accountant: "Hi Sarah, I need you to wire $15,000 to our new supplier for the Melbourne project ASAP. - [Your name]"

Why it works: Uses real names, projects, and relationships found on social media or company websites.

Defense: Verify all financial requests through a second communication method.

Business Email Compromise (BEC)

What it is: Criminals impersonate executives or suppliers to request money transfers.

Example: "This is [CEO name]. I'm in a confidential deal. Wire $50,000 to this account immediately. Don't discuss with anyone."

Target: Finance teams, accounts payable staff, business owners.

Defense: Always verify large payment requests with a phone call using known numbers.

Pharming

What it is: Redirecting you to fake websites even when you type the correct web address.

How: Malware on your computer or compromised DNS servers redirect legitimate sites to fake ones.

Example: You type "mybank.com" but get redirected to a fake banking site that steals your login.

Defense: Use bookmarks for important sites, check for HTTPS, keep antivirus updated.

Social Media Phishing

What it is: Fake messages or posts on LinkedIn, Facebook, or other platforms.

Example: LinkedIn message: "I saw your business profile. We can help you get a government grant. Click here for details."

Business angle: Often targets business opportunities, networking, or professional services.

Defense: Be skeptical of unsolicited business opportunities online.

Angler Phishing

What it is: Fake customer service accounts on social media responding to complaints.

Example: You complain about a service on Twitter. A fake support account replies: "DM us your login details to fix this."

Defense: Only communicate with verified official accounts. Check for blue verification ticks.

Whaling

What it is: Sophisticated attacks specifically targeting senior executives and business owners.

Example: Fake legal subpoena or tax audit notice requiring immediate response and sensitive information.

Why dangerous: Executives have access to the most sensitive information and financial controls.

Defense: Extra verification for all legal, tax, or regulatory requests.

Universal Red Flags (All Phishing Types)

These warning signs apply whether it's an email, call, text, or social media message:

Immediate Red Flags

  • Urgency/Pressure: "Act now or face consequences"
  • Fear tactics: "Your account is compromised/suspended"
  • Too good to be true: "You've won money" or "Amazing business opportunity"
  • Requests for sensitive info: Passwords, SSNs, banking details
  • Unusual requests: Boss asking for gift cards or wire transfers
  • Generic greetings: "Dear Customer" instead of your name
  • Mismatched information: Email from CEO but sent from Gmail account

The VERIFY Protocol

Before responding to any suspicious communication, follow this protocol:

V - VERIFY the Sender

Check email addresses, phone numbers, and social media accounts carefully. Look for small differences like "amaz0n.com" instead of "amazon.com".

E - EXAMINE the Request

Does this request make sense? Is it normal for this person/company to contact you this way?

R - RESIST Pressure

Legitimate companies rarely demand immediate action. Take time to think and verify.

I - INVESTIGATE Independently

Contact the company/person through official channels you already know (don't use contact info from the suspicious message).

F - FOLLOW UP Safely

If it's legitimate, they'll understand your caution. If it's fake, you've avoided a trap.

Y - YIELD to Caution

When in doubt, don't respond. It's better to miss one legitimate email than fall for one scam.

Real Attack Examples (Learn from Others' Mistakes)

Example: AI Voice Cloning Attack

What happened: A Sydney law firm's finance manager received a call from someone who sounded exactly like the senior partner, requesting an urgent $85,000 transfer for a "confidential acquisition."

The trick: Scammers used 3 seconds of audio from the partner's podcast interview to clone his voice with AI.

What saved them: The firm had a policy requiring all transfers over $10,000 to be verified with a second person in the room.

Lesson: Voice cloning is now easy and cheap. Never authorize large payments based on voice alone.

If Someone Falls for It

If you or someone in your business gets caught by a phishing attack, act fast:

First 10 Minutes

  1. Stop what you're doing, don't enter any more information
  2. Take screenshots of the phishing message/website for evidence
  3. Disconnect the affected device from the internet
  4. Alert your team that you may have been compromised

First Hour

  1. Change all important passwords (use a different device)
  2. Check bank accounts and credit cards for unauthorised activity
  3. Run antivirus/anti-malware scans on affected devices
  4. Contact your bank if financial information was compromised
  5. Enable fraud alerts on credit cards

First Day

  1. Report the incident
  2. Notify your cyber insurance provider if you have coverage
  3. Review and update your incident response plan
  4. Educate staff about this specific attack type
  5. Consider professional cybersecurity assessment

Building Your Defense Strategy

  • ✓ Train all staff on the VERIFY protocol
  • ✓ Create verification procedures for financial requests
  • ✓ Use multi-factor authentication on all important accounts
  • ✓ Keep software and security systems updated
  • ✓ Limit public information about your business and staff
  • ✓ Regular phishing simulations and training updates
  • ✓ Encourage reporting of suspicious communications without fear
  • ✓ Have an incident response plan ready
