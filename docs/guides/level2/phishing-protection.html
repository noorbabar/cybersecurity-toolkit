Phishing Protection

What is Phishing?

Phishing is when cyber criminals pretend to be someone you trust like your bank, a supplier, or a popular service, to trick you into handing over sensitive information.

Example: You receive an email that looks like it's from your bank saying, "Your account is locked, click here to unlock it." But the link goes to a fake website built to steal your password.

95% of successful cyber attacks start with a phishing email. And with AI, these fake emails are getting harder to spot, many have perfect spelling and grammar.

Top 5 Red Flags to Watch For

These are signs an email might be fake. Every small business should learn to recognise them:

1. Urgency or Pressure

Examples: "URGENT! Account suspended! Immediate action required!"

Why: Criminals use panic to make you act fast without thinking.

2. Suspicious Sender Email

Examples: "security@amaz0n.com" or "admin-paypal@gmail.com"

Check: Always check the sender's full email address. Is it legit?

3. Requests for Sensitive Info

Examples: Asking for passwords, credit card details, or ID numbers.

Reminder: No genuine company will ask for sensitive info via email.

4. Unexpected Attachments

Risky files: .zip, .exe, .doc from unknown senders.

Rule: If you didn’t expect it, don’t open it.

5. Strange or Misleading Links

Tip: Hover over links (don’t click) to see the real web address.

Red flag: The text says one thing, the actual link goes somewhere else.

Note: Typos used to be a sign, but not anymore. With AI, scammers write near-perfect emails. Focus on the red flags above.

Fake Emails to Learn From

Example: Fake Payment Request

From: ceo@your-business.com

Message: “Please wire $12,500 to our new supplier urgently. I’m in a meeting, don’t call me just send it.”

Why it's fake: Always verify large financial requests another way (e.g. phone call).

Example: Fake Microsoft Security Alert

From: security@m1crosoft-support.com

Message: “Unusual sign in activity. Click here to secure your account or risk deactivation.”

Why it's fake: Microsoft doesn’t use threats. Check the real website instead of clicking links.

The STOP Rule

Before clicking anything in an email, follow the STOP rule:

S – STOP

Don’t rush. Pause before clicking.

T – THINK

Were you expecting this email? Does the request make sense?

O – OBSERVE

Check the sender’s address. Hover over links to inspect them.

P – PROTECT

When in doubt, delete the email or contact the company yourself using official contact details.

If Someone Falls for It

If you or someone in your business clicks a bad link, act fast:

  1. Close the browser immediately
  2. Don't enter any information
  3. Change important passwords (email, banking, etc.)
  4. Run a virus/malware scan on your device
  5. Monitor accounts for unusual activity

Important: Encourage people to report mistakes, the sooner you act, the less damage is done.

Quick Steps for Any Business

  • ✓ Learn the 5 phishing red flags
  • ✓ Teach staff or partners to follow the STOP method
  • ✓ Create a simple plan for reporting suspicious emails
  • ✓ Don’t rely on spelling errors to spot phishing, check links and addresses
  • ✓ When in doubt, don’t click, verify directly
