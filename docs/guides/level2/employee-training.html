Employee Training

What is Employee Training?

Employee Training teaches your team how to spot and avoid cyber attacks. Your staff are often the first line of defense against criminals trying to break into your business systems.

Most successful cyber attacks start by tricking employees, not by breaking through technical defenses. Training helps your team become a security asset instead of a security risk.

Why Your Staff Are Targeted

  • Easier than hacking: It's simpler to trick a person than break through firewalls and security software
  • They have access: Employees already have legitimate access to your systems and data
  • They trust easily: Staff want to be helpful and may not question requests that seem to come from colleagues or suppliers
  • They make mistakes: Everyone clicks wrong links, uses weak passwords, or shares information when they shouldn't

Essential Training Topics

  • Phishing emails: How to spot fake emails trying to steal passwords or install malware
  • Password security: Creating strong passwords and never sharing login details
  • Physical security: Locking computers, securing documents, and controlling building access
  • Social engineering: Recognizing phone calls and in-person attempts to trick them into giving away information
  • Incident reporting: Who to tell and what to do when something suspicious happens
  • Safe internet use: Avoiding dangerous websites and downloads

Simple Training Methods

  • Monthly team meetings: Spend 10 minutes each month discussing a different security topic
  • Email examples: Forward real phishing emails (safely) to show what to watch for
  • Simple checklists: Give each employee a desk card with security reminders
  • Practice drills: Test how staff handle suspicious emails or phone calls
  • Real examples: Share news stories about local businesses that got attacked
  • Reward good behavior: Acknowledge staff who report suspicious activity

What to Teach About Modern Phishing (AI Era)

  • Perfect spelling means nothing: AI writes flawless emails now. Don't trust emails just because they're well-written
  • Check the actual email address: Click on the sender name to see the full email address. Scammers use similar domains like "gmai1.com" instead of "gmail.com"
  • Verify through different channels: If someone emails asking for money or information, call them on a number you already have saved
  • Be extra suspicious of urgency: "Wire this money today or we lose the contract" is a classic AI-generated pressure tactic
  • Watch for unusual requests: If your boss never usually emails you directly about payments, question it
  • Use our verification templates: We provide scripts for checking suspicious requests

Real World Example: AI Phishing Nearly Costs $80K

Case: A Melbourne law firm received a perfectly written email from their "accountant" requesting urgent payment to a new supplier for court documents.

AI tricks used: Perfect grammar, referenced real case names, used the accountant's email signature, created fake urgency about court deadlines.

What saved them: The office manager used their verification rule: she called the accountant on his mobile to confirm. It was fake.

Lesson: Even perfect emails need verification. AI makes everything look legitimate now.

Making Training Stick

  • Keep it short: 10-15 minute sessions work better than long presentations
  • Make it relevant: Use examples that relate to your specific business and industry
  • Be positive: Focus on helping staff protect the business rather than threatening punishment
  • Practice regularly: Monthly reminders work better than annual training sessions
  • Lead by example: Management should follow the same security practices they expect from staff
  • Update content: Keep training current with new threats and attack methods
