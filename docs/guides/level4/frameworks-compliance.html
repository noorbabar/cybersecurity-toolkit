Frameworks & Compliance

What This Is About

Frameworks and compliance are structured approaches to cybersecurity that help you organise your security efforts and meet legal requirements. Think of them as proven blueprints that tell you exactly what to do and in what order.

These frameworks help small businesses build professional grade security without guessing what's important or wasting money on unnecessary tools.

NIST Cybersecurity Framework

The NIST framework organises cybersecurity into five simple functions that work together:

1. Identify → Know what you have

What it means: Make lists of your important data, systems, and processes.

What to do:

  • List all computers, phones, and devices
  • Identify where your important data is stored
  • Document your critical business processes
  • Know who has access to what

2. Protect → Put safeguards in place

What it means: Implement security controls to prevent attacks.

What to do:

  • Use strong passwords and two-factor authentication - read more here
  • Train your staff on security - read more here
  • Keep software updated - read more here
  • Back up your data regularly - read more here

3. Detect → Find problems quickly

What it means: Set up monitoring to catch cyber attacks early.

What to do:

  • Enable login alerts on important accounts
  • Monitor for unusual activity
  • Set up automatic security scanning
  • Train staff to report suspicious activity

4. Respond → Take action when attacked

What it means: Have a plan for what to do during a cyber attack.

What to do:

  • Create an incident response plan - read more here
  • Know who to call for help
  • Practice your response procedures
  • Document everything that happens

5. Recover → Get back to normal

What it means: Restore your systems and learn from the incident.

What to do:

  • Test your backup recovery procedures
  • Have a business continuity plan
  • Review what went wrong and improve
  • Update your security based on lessons learned

CIS Controls (Implementation Group 1)

The CIS Controls are prioritised security actions. Small businesses should focus on Implementation Group 1:

Top 6 CIS Controls for small business:

  1. Inventory your assets: List every device that connects to your network
  2. Inventory your software: Document all programs and remove unused ones
  3. Protect your data: Encrypt sensitive information and control access
  4. Secure configurations: Change default passwords and disable unnecessary services
  5. Manage user accounts: Control who has access to what systems
  6. Control access: Limit permissions to only what people need for their jobs

Implementation roadmap:

  • Week 1-2: Complete asset inventory using a simple spreadsheet
  • Week 3-4: Audit and remove unused software
  • Month 2: Implement data encryption on all devices
  • Month 3: Apply security configurations to all systems
  • Month 4: Review and clean up user accounts and permissions

Privacy Compliance (Australian Privacy Act)

If your business has annual turnover over $3 million or handles health information, you must comply with privacy laws:

Key privacy requirements:

  1. Have a privacy policy: Explain how you collect, use, and protect personal information
  2. Collect only what you need: Don't gather unnecessary personal data
  3. Tell people why you need their information: Be transparent about your data collection
  4. Protect personal information: Use reasonable security measures to keep data safe
  5. Give people access to their data: Let customers see what information you have about them
  6. Fix incorrect information: Update personal data when people show you it's wrong

Data breach notification:

If you have a serious data breach, you must:

  • Report to the Office of the Australian Information Commissioner within 72 hours
  • Notify affected individuals as soon as possible
  • Include what happened, what data was involved, and what people should do
  • Keep records of all breaches for at least two years

Risk Assessment Methods

Formal risk assessment helps you make informed security decisions:

Simple risk assessment process:

  1. Identify your assets: List everything valuable in your business
  2. Identify threats: What could attack or damage your assets
  3. Find vulnerabilities: What weaknesses could threats exploit
  4. Calculate impact: How much damage would each successful attack cause
  5. Estimate likelihood: How likely is each threat to succeed
  6. Prioritise risks: Focus on high-impact, high-likelihood scenarios first

Risk rating system:

  • Critical: High impact + High likelihood → Act immediately
  • High: High impact + Medium likelihood → Act within 30 days
  • Medium: Medium impact + Medium likelihood → Act within 90 days
  • Low: Low impact regardless of likelihood → Act when resources allow

Real-World Example: Framework Implementation Success

What happened: A Melbourne accounting firm with 25 employees was struggling with multiple security issues - weak passwords, no backup procedures, and untrained staff clicking malicious links.

What they did: They implemented the NIST framework systematically over 6 months, starting with asset inventory and moving through each function.

The results: Within a year, they had zero successful phishing attacks, recovered from a ransomware attempt in 2 hours using their backups, and passed their professional liability insurance cybersecurity audit.

Key lesson: Following a structured framework was more effective than randomly implementing security tools.

Source: ACSC Small Business Cyber Security Guide

Choosing Your Framework

Different frameworks work better for different businesses:

Framework selection guide:

  • NIST Framework: Best for comprehensive security management across all business types
  • CIS Controls: Best for businesses wanting specific, prioritised actions
  • Privacy compliance: Required for businesses over $3M turnover or handling health data
  • Risk assessment: Essential foundation for any security program

Implementation approach:

  1. Start with risk assessment: Understand your biggest threats first
  2. Choose one framework: Don't try to implement multiple frameworks simultaneously
  3. Focus on fundamentals: Get basic security controls working before advanced features
  4. Document everything: Keep records of what you've implemented and why
  5. Review regularly: Update your approach as your business grows

Framework Benefits for Small Business

  • Structured approach: Know exactly what to do and in what order
  • Proven effectiveness: Based on thousands of organisations' experiences
  • Cost efficiency: Focus spending on highest impact security measures
  • Compliance readiness: Meet legal and insurance requirements
  • Professional credibility: Demonstrate serious commitment to security
