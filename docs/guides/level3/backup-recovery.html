Data Backup & Recovery

Why Backup Your Data?

Backing up your data means making extra copies of your important files and information so you don't lose everything if your computer crashes, gets stolen, or ransomware locks you out.

It's one of the simplest ways to protect your business and keep running even when things go wrong.

The 3-2-1 Backup Rule

Follow this simple rule to protect your data properly:

  • 3 copies of your data: Your original file plus 2 backup copies
  • 2 different types of storage: One on your computer/server, one on an external drive or cloud
  • 1 copy stored offsite: Cloud storage or external drive kept somewhere else (like your home)

This way, even if your office burns down or gets flooded, you still have your data safe somewhere else.

Step-by-Step Implementation

  • Step 1 - List your critical data: Customer database, financial records, contracts, employee records, and any files you can't afford to lose
  • Step 2 - Choose cloud backup: Sign up for Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, or Dropbox Business. Set it to automatically sync your important folders
  • Step 3 - Get external storage: Buy a 1TB external hard drive for $100-150. Use Windows Backup or Mac Time Machine to copy files weekly
  • Step 4 - Set up automatic backups: Configure your computer to back up to both cloud and external drive without you remembering
  • Step 5 - Test monthly: Try restoring one file from each backup location to make sure it actually works
  • Step 6 - Store offsite copy: Keep the external drive at home or use a second cloud service

Specific Tools and Costs

  • Cloud Storage Options: Google Workspace ($9/month), Microsoft 365 ($8.25/month), or Dropbox Business ($15/month per user)
  • External Drives: Seagate or Western Digital 1TB portable drive ($100-150)
  • Backup Software: Use built-in Windows Backup, Mac Time Machine, or paid options like Acronis ($50/year)
  • Total monthly cost: Usually $10-20 per month for small businesses
  • Recovery time: Cloud backups restore in hours, external drives in minutes

What to Back Up (Priority Order)

  • Priority 1: Customer database, financial records, and contracts
  • Priority 2: Employee records, inventory data, and current projects
  • Priority 3: Email archives, marketing materials, and older documents
  • Don't back up: Software programs (you can reinstall these), temporary files, or anything you can easily recreate

Example: When Backups Save the Day

Case: In 2019 the City of Baltimore was hit by ransomware, locking many systems and stopping key services. Limited backups meant recovery took weeks and cost over $18 million.

Impact: Services were disrupted, payments delayed, and public trust was damaged.

Fix: Baltimore has since improved backup and recovery plans to prevent future disasters.

Source: 2019 Baltimore Ransomware Attack

Start Backing Up This Week

  • ☐ Write down your top 10 most important files and folders
  • ☐ Sign up for Google Workspace or Microsoft 365 and install the sync software
  • ☐ Buy a 1TB external hard drive from JB Hi-Fi or Officeworks
  • ☐ Set up Windows Backup or Mac Time Machine to run automatically every night
  • ☐ Test restoring one file from both your cloud and external backup
  • ☐ Schedule monthly backup tests in your calendar
  • ☐ Store your external drive somewhere other than your office

Following the 3-2-1 rule means ransomware, theft, or disasters can't destroy your business data.
