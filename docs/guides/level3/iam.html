Managing Staff & Access

What This Is About

Managing staff and access means controlling who can get into your business systems and training your team to spot cyber threats. Your employees are both your biggest security risk and your strongest defense, it all depends on how well you manage and train them.

Most successful cyber attacks target employees rather than breaking through technical defenses, making staff management and training critical for small business security.

The AAA Framework (Access Control Basics)

Every business needs to follow three key steps for controlling access to systems and data:

Authentication - Who are you?

What it means: Confirming someone is who they claim to be before letting them in.

What to do:

  • Require strong passwords for all accounts
  • Use two-factor authentication, we'll cover this in detail: read more here
  • Never share login credentials between people
  • Change passwords immediately when staff leave

Authorisation: What can you do?

What it means: Deciding what someone is allowed to access once they're logged in.

What to do:

  • Give people only the minimum access they need for their job
  • Limit admin privileges to essential staff only
  • Review and update permissions when roles change
  • Remove access to sensitive data for temporary workers

Accounting: What did you do?

What it means: Keeping records of who accessed what and when.

What to do:

  • Enable login logs on important systems
  • Monitor for unusual access patterns
  • Keep records of permission changes
  • Review logs monthly for suspicious activity

Employee Security Training

What your team needs to know:

  1. How to spot phishing emails: Look for urgent language, spelling mistakes, and requests for passwords or money
  2. Password security: We'll show them how to create strong passwords: read more here
  3. Physical security: Lock computers when stepping away and don't let strangers into the office
  4. Social engineering: Be suspicious of phone calls asking for sensitive information
  5. When to ask for help: Create a culture where staff feel comfortable reporting suspicious activity

Training methods that work:

  1. Monthly 10 minute team meetings: Cover one security topic each month
  2. Real examples: Share news stories about local businesses that got attacked
  3. Simple desk cards: Give each employee a card with security reminders
  4. Practice scenarios: Test how staff handle suspicious emails or phone calls
  5. Reward good behavior: Acknowledge staff who report suspicious activity

Modern phishing training (AI era):

Today's phishing attacks use AI to create perfect emails with no spelling mistakes. Train your team to:

  • Always verify unusual requests through a different communication method
  • Check email addresses carefully, scammers use similar domains
  • Be extra suspicious of urgency and pressure tactics
  • Question requests that seem out of character for the sender

Managing Admin Privileges

Why limit admin access:

Admin privileges let someone control everything on your computers and systems. If a cyber criminal gets admin access, they can steal all your data, install malware, or lock you out completely.

What to do:

  1. Find who has admin access: Check user accounts on all computers and systems
  2. Remove unnecessary admin rights: Only business owners and essential IT staff should have admin access
  3. Use standard accounts for daily work: Even admins should use regular accounts for email and normal tasks
  4. Secure admin accounts: Use strong passwords and two-factor authentication
  5. Monitor admin activity: Keep logs of when admin accounts are used and for what
  6. Remove access immediately: When someone leaves, remove their admin access the same day

Setting up user controls:

  1. Create role-based access: Group similar jobs together with the same permissions
  2. Use the principle of least privilege: Give people only what they need, nothing more
  3. Review access quarterly: Check who has access to what every three months
  4. Document everything: Keep records of who has access to what and why
  5. Test your controls: Try accessing systems with different user accounts to verify restrictions work

Real-World Example: Insider Threat Prevented

What happened: A Perth marketing agency discovered that a contractor was accessing client files outside their project scope, potentially to steal client lists for a competing business.

How it was caught: The business had implemented access logging and noticed the contractor was logging in during weekends and accessing files unrelated to their assigned project.

What they did: They immediately revoked the contractor's access, reviewed what files were accessed, and implemented stricter access controls with project specific permissions.

The lesson: Proper access controls and monitoring caught the threat before any data was stolen or misused.

Source: ACSC Small Business Cyber Security Guide

Building Your Access Management System

Here's what you need to implement comprehensive staff and access management:

Essential components:

  • User account management: Create accounts for each person with appropriate permissions
  • Password policies: We'll show you how to implement strong password requirements: read more here
  • Multi-factor authentication: Add extra security layers: read more here
  • Regular training: Keep your team updated on the latest threats and procedures
  • Access reviews: Regularly check and update who has access to what

Key Management Principles

  • Start with people: Train your team before implementing complex technical controls
  • Limit access: Give people only what they need to do their job
  • Monitor activity: Keep logs of who accesses what and when
  • Remove access quickly: When someone leaves, remove their access immediately
  • Review regularly: Check permissions and training effectiveness quarterly
