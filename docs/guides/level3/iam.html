Identity and Access Management (IDAM)

What is Identity and Access Management?

Identity and Access Management means controlling who can get into your business systems and data. It makes sure only the right people can see or change important information.

This stops cyber criminals from sneaking in using stolen passwords or leftover accounts from people who no longer work with you.

The AAA of Access Management

To keep your business safe, focus on the three key parts of access control, known as AAA:

  • Authentication: Confirming who someone is — usually by requiring a username and password, or better, adding a second step like a code sent to a phone (MFA).
  • Authorization: Deciding what that person is allowed to do. For example, a staff member might view customer info but not delete it.
  • Accounting: Keeping records of who accessed what and when — so you can check if anything suspicious happens.

Using these three steps helps you control access carefully and spot problems early.

How to Manage Identity and Access

  • Review who has access: Make a list of everyone with accounts and what they can do.
  • Remove old accounts: Delete or disable accounts for former staff, contractors, or temporary users.
  • Use strong passwords: Require passwords that are hard to guess, and change them regularly.
  • Turn on Multi Factor Authentication (MFA): Require a second check like a code on a phone whenever someone logs in.
  • Limit access: Give people only the access they need to do their job, not more.
  • Keep logs: Where possible, track who logged in and what they did to help catch problems early.

Real World Example: When Access Is Not Managed

Case: A small Australian healthcare provider was hit by ransomware after a staff computer without admin restrictions was compromised.

Impact: Systems were locked and patients turned away. The business shut down for five days and costs went over fifty thousand dollars.

Fix: They now limit admin access to the owner, use automatic updates, and perform backups every night.

Source: ACSC Small Business Cyber Security Guide

Simple Steps to Start Today

  • ☐ List all user accounts and their permissions
  • ☐ Remove access for anyone who no longer needs it
  • ☐ Require strong passwords and change them every 3 to 6 months
  • ☐ Turn on Multi Factor Authentication for all important accounts
  • ☐ Limit admin rights only to trusted people who really need it
  • ☐ Where possible, enable logging to track user activity
